It was particularly the month of November in 2022 when the world witnessed one of the best LLMs to be released for public use. It was the day when Sam Altman from OpenAI dropped his tweet launching ChatGPT for the masses. Within hours, my entire YouTube feed was overwhelmed with the reviews of this tool and I couldn’t comprehend what exactly had happened. To this, I skimmed through YouTube seeing its use cases and capabilities. It was the time which got me awestruck by the new product space we were entering. Who would have thought that something like ChatGPT would become the everyday tool of the masses after just a few years after its launch?

Like every tech enthusiast, I hopped onto their website and exploited the daily free limits every day. It felt like magic. Something so personalised and contextualised was something that we all were waiting for. You ask it anything, it replies the exact same answer to your question. It wasn’t something like another browser where you’d have to manually skim through all those endless links to find the exact thing what you needed. Also, it was great at remembering the context of the chat and following up with recurring discussions. The outputs that it produced initially weren’t that accurate; it did hallucinate a lot in the early times, but as new models piled up, their prediction ability also blew up exceptionally. For a 10th grader, it was more than enough. I never really tried it for completing my assignments, but the research it did was really very helpful. I hardly turned to Google till then for nuanced tasks like fact-checking or basic research.

Then came my 11th and 12th when I started prepping for JEE. I remember those days when I used to feed entire contexts regarding my scores and study patterns into GPT to map my studies, ask for recommendations, and generate countless timetables which I kept tweaking every week.

Slowly, the Gen AI wave had hit the mainstream markets with so many new tools coming up. Every content that I consumed after then was something related to AI or an allied domain. We were gradually seeing the changes and shift in industries actually taking place after so many years of development in this sector. It apparently happened to look like all of a sudden we were hit by a really tectonic shift in software, but it wasn’t. It was the consequence of all the previous decades' worth of research being implemented and given a product form actually unfolding. For those who were ignorant, they continued to stay so and happened to watch all these investments tanking up the AI-first companies.

During this period, we also saw NVIDIA becoming the world’s most valuable company due to its GPU compliance in running the AI ecosystems. We saw tools like Midjourney and DALL-E coming up and revolutionising the entire content creation industry. One of the most well-thought hypotheses in the domain of AI was that creative tasks like artworks and related stuff would be the last to be introduced to this, as these require some higher level of architecture to mimic human levels of thought patterns—but this industry was one of the most initial frontiers that were hit with it. I spent countless hours during my preparation trying out these exceptional works of art myself. What I mostly encountered was that, as these were newly released, many of them had huge paywalls and stricter rate limits which made them less accessible to me.

Then came the open-source wave with Meta launching its Llama 2 & 3, which pushed the concept of local hosting of AI models. During that time, I felt the need to have better hardware as these models were terribly loathsome on the RAM and compute. What I did was visit one of the local arcade shops that had some good PCs lined up solely for the purpose of playing games. What I did next was actually try to download and run the model locally, and it didn’t go as planned. Referring to a GitHub repo was not actually a good plan to implement this indeed. Then I saw one of the tutorials that used Ollama instead to host the model in a much easier and reliable way. The next time I did the same, downloaded a 4-bit quantized 7B Llama-2 version, and it did work! It wasn’t as “smart” as GPT and lacked terribly in the response time, but it did feel crazy having no rate limits.

Ever since then, I kept digging up and exploring this domain. Though I had not hit up to the technical aspects of these technologies, what happened was I did implement a top-to-bottom layer approach. Starting from the absolute tip of the iceberg (the real product) to understanding the nuances and aspects involved in building those. The compute urgency, the implementations, the RAM crisis, the chip crisis—everything that was happening, I came to understand those effects and causes. My whole JEE prep went into juggling through classes and exploring this stuff.

During my last phases of the preparation journey, all I could think of was when all of this would get over and I would actually happen to learn about the awesome stuff happening right now. Somehow it felt that I was lagging behind by not learning about it during my preparation, but that was worth it as grabbing a seat at a decent college is considered equally valuable in India. In a way, it did motivate me to study so as to secure a seat at a decent college where I could actually build these things someday hands-on. Though not fully, but partially, I did achieve this goal. I happened to secure a seat at a national-level college (not IIT ofc).
